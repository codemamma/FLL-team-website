UNEARTHED (2025–26)

Archaeology · History · Preservation

Inspired by the UNEARTHED™ challenge, our team explored how discoveries from the past can shape a better future. We were fascinated by how artifacts tell stories about human innovation, culture, and problem-solving across time. Our goal was to design a solution that helps preserve, understand, and share historical discoveries using modern STEM tools.

The Problem

Archaeologists rely heavily on electronic devices such as tablets and iPads to take photos, scan for dig sites, and record notes in the field. However, these devices often become difficult or impossible to use due to water droplets, dust, overheating in hot weather, and reduced battery performance in cold conditions. In some cases, devices must be turned off for long periods, which slows down important fieldwork and can lead to lost data.

Our Solution

We designed a protective device case that helps archaeologists keep their screens usable and their devices functioning in challenging environments. Our solution includes a screen-wiping mechanism to remove water and dust, a durable carbon-fiber body, and a metal plate inside the case to help transfer heat away from the device. In later iterations, we added a thermal material (such as paraffin) to help regulate temperature—absorbing heat in hot conditions and releasing it in cold conditions to protect battery life.

Research & Feedback

We researched how archaeologists work and the challenges they face in real field conditions. Our research included: * Learning how archaeologists use devices for documentation and discovery * Studying why water droplets reduce touch sensitivity on screens * Investigating existing solutions such as micro-vibration drying, cooling cases, and screen-cleaning tools * Reviewing expert resources like Ask an Archaeologist: Tech Edition * Conducting experiments to test different screen-cleaning methods and device orientations We also built and iterated multiple prototypes using sketches and TinkerCAD models to test durability, cleaning effectiveness, and temperature control.

Impact & Learning

We gathered feedback from archaeologists, anthropologists, educators, and community members, including expert feedback from professionals in archaeology and digital asset management. We also collected survey data from over 25 people, which showed that many individuals outside archaeology experience similar problems with water, dust, overheating, and battery life. Based on this feedback, we continued iterating our design to improve durability, temperature regulation, and usability, and identified screen brightness and battery preservation as key areas for future improvement.
